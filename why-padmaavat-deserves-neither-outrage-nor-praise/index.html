Why Padmaavat Deserves Neither Outrage Nor Praise

Padmaavat gave analysis paralysis to the entire nation. Now being lauded as the biggest hit of 2018, so far, Padmaavat had a very bumpy start.

Sanjay Leela Bhansali has had the dream of making Padmaavat for almost a decade. He poured a lot of money, 1.9 billion rupees to be exact, into this project.

Just before the release of Padmaavat, however, the Rajput Karni Sena sprouted up from the ground making really ugly threats against the movie and violent attacks on the movie sets and cast. The protests forced the CBFC to take a hard long look at Padmaavat, which delayed the release of the movie from December 2017 to January 2018 and ultimately resulted in a change of the title from Padmaavati to Padmaavat . So we walked into the 18th year of the 21st century, going crazy over a movie based on a 16 th century poem based on a 14th century queen, who, never existed in the first place.

Padmaavat became a symbolic hero of sorts as it emerged from the muck that it was dragged into. Several celebrities came out in the support of the movie and expressed outrage over the current state of affairs.

But if you watch the movie, you realize that the movie is undeserving of the offense and unworthy of praise.

The only people who should take offense at Padmaavat are the two characters who actually existed - Allaudin Khalji and Rawal Ratan Simha.

Allaudin Khalji was the second and the most powerful ruler of the Khalji dynasty that ruled the Delhi Sultante in India. Under his reign, the delhi sultanat expanded manifold.

Khalji systematically and brutally attacked the Rajputs of Rajasthan and Gujarat, first for plunder and later for annexation. It is understandable that the Rajput princes saw him as a barbaric foreigner looting their wealth and women. The Khalji threat was real and the rajput men fought tooth and nail to their last breath. They were flesh and blood.

But for a director lost in the beauty of his own creation, Padmaavat becomes a menagerie of cardboard caricatures. There is no depth to the characters, under the highly polished veneer.

Allaudin Khalji is reduced to a barbarian with an unsatiable apetite - where as by all accounts Khalji who is credited with systematic invasion of India, repeated thwarting of mongols and who constructed several monuments and introduced a complicated tax code would have been one of the most sophisticated man of his times.

Raja Ratan Singh on the other hand, has been portrayed as a smokey pouting doll who utters a platitude about Rajput bravery every time someone pushed his button

Don't get me wrong. The offense taken by the Karni sena is unfounded, firstly because this is a fictional adaptation of a fictional adaptation. If anything, they should be glad for the stereotype of rajput life this movie promotes.

Padmaavat fails as a movie because the costumes and jewelry are better designed than its characters.

The lack of a story is obvious even from the trailers which feature hardly 10 seconds of dialogue. A few years ago, this would have been a justified effort simply for the stunning visuals, but in post-Bahubali era of cinema, there is no A for effort.

This movie has beautiful bones but is an empty shell at best.
